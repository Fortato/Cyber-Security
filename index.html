SHORTAGE OF CYBERSECURITY WORKFORCE
Evidence of the cybersecurity workforce shortage continues to mount: By 2022, there will be 1.8 million open jobs in this field, according to the recent Global Information Security Workforce Study. This is up from the 2015 estimate of 1.5 million by 2020. "The numbers are going in the wrong direction," said
Wesley Simpson, COO of ISC(2), of the study, which surveyed 19,000 cyber security professionals. "It's a huge concern to organizations as well as people in this profession."



REASON FOR SHORTAGE
One major reason for the shortage? Millennials aren't going into the field. Only 7% of cyber security workers surveyed were under age 29, and 13% were between ages 30 and 34. The average age of cyber professionals is 42, Simpson said. "Over the next 10 years, we will have a large population of cyber professionals starting to retire," Simpson said. "We don't have a good plan to backfill those large numbers of folks starting to leave the industry. We need to be able to educate and bring awareness to all facets of cyber security, and send a message that regardless of if you have a technical degree or not, it's a great, diverse, lucrative career for folks to get into."
The reason it's difficult to attract millennials to the field is largely due to their lack of awareness, Simpson said. Cyber security is often not on the radar of young children as a potential career, or as being different from general IT work.
"This is a field in great demand, with 2% unemployment globally, and an average salary of about $100,000. We're not doing a good job educating folks about it," said Simpson.
COLLEGE DROPOUTS
While computer science occupations are growing, the college graduation rates in the United States for STEM-related degrees are one of the lowest ratios of bachelor’s degrees in the world. According to the U.S. Department of Education, National Center for Education Statistics, 48 percent of beginning bachelor’s degree students in STEM fields left their field before graduating. HIGH SCHOOL PLANS
The Alliance for California Computing Education for Students and Schools (ACCESS) cites that 9 out of 10 high school students will not get any computer science skills before they graduate from high school. With the call for national efforts to improve STEM-related performance, the High School Career department will be focusing on aligning a K-12 computer science education with college-level degree programs, providing a rigorous computer science education to meet the demands of these computer science and STEM-related job openings and to compete in a global economy.
